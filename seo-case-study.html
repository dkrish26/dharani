How We Made 30,000 Product Pages Discoverable on Google

Company Magrudy Enterprises LLC Platform Next.js / Vercel Timeline Jan 2026 – Jul 2026 Role Diagnosed & implemented solo

Magrudy's had over 30,000 products in its catalogue. Google could see almost none of them. Daily organic visits were stuck below 1,000. The fix turned out to be a handful of files — but finding the problem required understanding exactly how Google reads a Next.js site.

The Problem

Magrudy's e-commerce site was built on Next.js deployed on Vercel. On the surface it looked fine, pages loaded, products appeared, checkout worked. But organic search traffic was stubbornly low for a retailer with 30,000 SKUs competing in the UAE market against Amazon.ae and Noon.

A routine check of Google Search Console revealed the scale of the issue: Google had indexed barely 15,000–17,000 pages and that number was declining, not growing. The site was effectively invisible for product-level searches.

"We had 30,000 products. Google could see roughly half of them — and that number was going in the wrong direction."

The Diagnosis

Fetching a product page without JavaScript the way Googlebot sees it revealed the problem immediately. The page returned navbar, footer, and a loading spinner. No product title. No price. No description. Digging into the codebase confirmed four compounding issues:

1. Product data fetched client-side

The product page had a getStaticProps function, Next.js's server-side mechanism but it only fetched layout elements. The actual product was fetched inside a useEffect hook in the browser. Google received an empty shell. The product existed in MongoDB and the API worked, but the data never reached the initial HTML response that Google reads.

2. One title tag for 30,000 pages

The title and meta description were hardcoded in the layout component and inherited by every page. Google treats 30,000 pages with identical titles as duplicate or thin content exactly the wrong signal for a product catalogue.

3. The sitemap was a placeholder

The sitemap file still contained commented-out tutorial code referencing jsonplaceholder.typicode.com. The live sitemap output exactly one URL: the homepage. Worse, robots.txt pointed Google to /sitemap.xml but the file served at /sitemap_1.xml meaning the referenced sitemap likely returned a 404.

Before: Two sitemaps submitted one discovering 1 page, the other 120. Out of 30,000+ products.

4. Zero structured data

No JSON-LD Product or Book schema anywhere in the codebase. No rich results eligibility, no Google Shopping signals, no price or availability data visible to search engines.

The Fix

Four files. No architecture change. The product data was already in MongoDB, the API route already called it server-side the issue was simply where in the Next.js lifecycle that call happened.

Fix 1
Moved product fetch into getStaticProps
Called GetItemByIsbn() server-side in [productId].js and passed the product as a prop. The page already had revalidate: 100 (ISR) so caching was handled. Google now receives the full product in the initial HTML.
Fix 2
Unique title and meta per product page
Added a Next.js Head block generating a unique title per product e.g. "Atomic Habits by James Clear | Buy Online UAE | Magrudy's". 30,000 unique pages instead of 30,000 identical ones.
Fix 3
Rewrote sitemap to query MongoDB directly
Replaced the placeholder with a sitemap that queries the item collection for all active ISBNs and generates a chunked sitemap index. Fixed robots.txt to point to the correct URL. Submitted to Search Console immediately.
Fix 4
Manual indexing for bestsellers
Used the URL Inspection tool in Search Console to manually request indexing for top-selling product pages rather than waiting the full 4–5 weeks for Googlebot to crawl everything organically.

The Results

The full crawl took 4–5 weeks but the graph tells the story. After a long plateau around 15,000–17,000 indexed pages, a sharp spike in early July as Google processed the new sitemap and server-rendered pages.

The indexing graph the spike around July 2nd is the fix taking effect. Indexed pages nearly doubled from ~17,000 to 34,782.
After: A single sitemap index discovering 67,597 URLs up from 121 total before the fix.
Search Console performance over 28 days, 29.47K clicks, 246.3K impressions, 9.6% CTR, average position 6.5.

"We didn't need to outspend Amazon on ads. We just needed Google to be able to read our pages, which it couldn't, until we fixed four files."

Key takeaways

  • Client-side data fetching is invisible to Google always verify what Googlebot actually receives
  • A single shared title tag across 30,000 pages is treated as duplicate content
  • A broken or placeholder sitemap means Google never discovers your URLs
  • Manual URL inspection in Search Console accelerates indexing for priority pages
  • SSR fixes in Next.js can be surgical no architecture overhaul required
